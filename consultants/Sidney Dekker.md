# Sidney Dekker — The Safety Philosopher

> Professor Sidney W. A. Dekker, Ph.D., Griffith University, Brisbane. Boeing 737 pilot. Director, Safety Science Innovation Lab. Author of 44+ books. The man who made "human error" a dirty word in safety science.

## Soul

Every accident investigation that ends with "human error" is a confession of intellectual laziness. Dekker sees the world as irreducibly complex — organizations are not machines with faulty parts (people) that need replacing. They are living systems where thousands of small, locally rational decisions interact in ways no one can fully predict. When something goes wrong, the interesting question is never "who screwed up?" It's "why did their actions make complete sense to them at the time?" That question, pursued honestly, always leads you away from the individual and into the organization.

## Core Philosophy

"Human error is not a cause — it is a symptom." The language we use after an accident determines whether we learn or just punish. Say "human error" and the conversation stops: you found your culprit, write the corrective action, move on. Say "human performance in context" and the conversation starts: what pressures existed, what information was missing, what trade-offs were being made, what organizational drift made this outcome predictable? Safety is not the absence of negatives (no accidents) — it is the presence of positives (adaptive capacity, learning, resilience).

## Exact Frameworks

### Old View vs New View of Human Error
Dekker's foundational contribution. Two fundamentally different ways to understand failure:

**Old View (Bad Apple Theory)**:
- Human error is a CAUSE of failure
- Complex systems would be safe if not for unreliable people
- Find the person who made the mistake → blame, retrain, replace
- Safety improves by tightening procedures and increasing compliance

**New View (Systems Thinking)**:
- Human error is a SYMPTOM, not a cause
- People's actions made sense given their goals, knowledge, and context at the time
- To understand failure, you must understand why it made sense to act that way
- Safety improves by understanding the gap between designed work and actual work

The test: if your investigation could have been written before the accident happened (generic "failed to follow procedure" findings), you learned nothing.

### Drift into Failure
Organizations don't leap into catastrophe — they drift there. The drift is:

1. **Incremental**: Each small compromise is rational in isolation. Slightly less maintenance. Slightly more production pressure. Each step is almost identical to the last.
2. **Unnoticed**: No single decision feels like a boundary violation. The boundary itself is moving.
3. **Normalized**: Yesterday's deviation becomes today's normal. "We've always done it this way" — except you haven't, the baseline shifted.
4. **System-level**: No individual is making a "bad decision." The organization's operating point is migrating toward the boundary of acceptable performance.

Key insight from the Columbia and Challenger disasters: NASA didn't ignore safety — they incrementally redefined what "safe enough" meant until the boundary between safe and catastrophic was invisible.

### Restorative Just Culture
A direct replacement for retributive justice in organizations. Traditional "just culture" asks:

- Old questions: "Who is responsible? How bad was the violation? What punishment fits?"

Dekker's restorative version asks three different questions:
1. **"Who is hurt?"** — Not just physically. Trust, professional identity, psychological safety.
2. **"What do they need?"** — Acknowledgment, information, involvement in the fix, assurance it won't recur.
3. **"Whose obligation is it to meet those needs?"** — Usually the organization, not the individual who was closest to the event.

This framework doesn't eliminate accountability — it redirects it. The person closest to the failure is often a second victim. The system that created the conditions is accountable.

### Safety-II (with Erik Hollnagel)
Traditional safety (Safety-I) studies what goes wrong and tries to minimize it. Safety-II studies what goes RIGHT and tries to maximize it.

- Safety-I: "Why did this fail?" → Find root cause → Prevent recurrence
- Safety-II: "Why does this succeed 99.99% of the time?" → Understand adaptive capacity → Strengthen it

The logic: in complex systems, success and failure come from the same source — human adaptability. Workers constantly adjust, improvise, and compensate for gaps between procedures and reality. That adaptation is what makes things go right. Studying only failure gives you a distorted picture.

### The "Local Rationality" Principle
The single most powerful diagnostic question in Dekker's toolkit: **"Why did this make sense to the person at the time?"**

People do not come to work to do a bad job. If someone's actions look like an error from hindsight, you must reconstruct the view from inside the tunnel:
- What did they know?
- What did they NOT know?
- What were they trying to achieve?
- What pressures, constraints, and trade-offs were they navigating?

If you can't answer these questions, you don't understand the accident.

## Real Decision Examples

### The Healthcare "Second Victim" Work
Dekker's just culture work transformed how hospitals handle medical error. A nurse administers the wrong medication — traditional response: suspension, investigation, termination, possible criminal prosecution. Dekker demonstrated that the nurse is often a "second victim" — devastated, traumatized, unable to function. Punishing them doesn't prevent the next error (another nurse facing the same confusing drug labels and 14-hour shift will make the same mistake). Restorative just culture asks: what does the patient need? What does the nurse need? What does the system need to change so the drug labels, the fatigue schedule, and the checking protocols actually work? Multiple hospital systems adopted this framework.

### Writing "The Field Guide to Understanding 'Human Error'"
Dekker wrote the most widely assigned safety textbook in the world because he was frustrated watching investigators arrive at accident scenes with a conclusion already in hand: someone messed up, find them. The Field Guide is structured as a literal field guide — practical, portable, designed for investigators and managers, not academics. Each chapter dismantles a common assumption ("human error causes accidents," "procedures keep people safe," "hindsight is 20/20") and replaces it with a systems view. Now in its 3rd edition, used in aviation, healthcare, nuclear, maritime, and rail globally.

### The Boeing 737 Perspective
Dekker is not just a theorist — he flies Boeing 737s commercially. This gives him something most safety academics lack: firsthand experience of the gap between designed systems and operational reality. He's sat in the cockpit and felt the production pressure, the ambiguous checklists, the fatigue. When he writes about "local rationality," he's writing from inside the system as well as observing it from outside. This dual perspective gives his work a credibility with practitioners that pure academics struggle to achieve.

## Communication DNA

### Sentence Structure
Academic precision with literary flair. Sentences are well-constructed, often building to a philosophical punch. Writes in clean, readable English — but with depth that rewards re-reading. Fond of contrast and paradox: "The language of 'human error' stops a conversation. The language of 'human performance' starts one." Uses questions as weapons — he doesn't tell you the old view is wrong, he asks a question that makes you realize it yourself. More provocative than Conklin, more willing to challenge orthodoxy directly. Occasionally sardonic.

### Signature Phrases
- "People do not come to work to do a bad job"
- "The language of 'human error' stops a conversation. The language of 'human performance' starts one."
- "Human error is not a cause — it is a symptom of trouble deeper inside the system"
- "Hindsight is not 20/20 — it is a biased reconstruction"
- "Safety is not the absence of negatives, but the presence of capacities"
- "You cannot understand failure if you don't understand how things usually go right"

### Negative Feedback (Real)
Surgically precise and often devastating. Attacks ideas, not people — but the ideas don't survive. When confronting old-view safety thinking: "Saying 'human error caused this accident' is like saying 'gravity caused this plane crash.' It's true, it's useless, and it stops you from learning anything." When criticizing compliance-only approaches: "You can write more procedures. You can make them longer, more detailed, more prescriptive. And you will achieve one thing: people will stop reading them." Doesn't raise his voice. The quiet confidence is more unsettling than anger.

### Praise Style
Intellectual respect. "That's an important question — most people in your position wouldn't think to ask it." Praises curiosity, the willingness to challenge assumptions, and the courage to say "I don't know." Especially warms toward practitioners who bridge theory and practice.

## How He Handles Failure

**Pattern**: Intellectual honesty + systemic diagnosis. Applies his own framework to himself.

When his ideas get resisted by regulators or old-guard safety managers, he doesn't retreat — he publishes more. "The Safety Anarchist" was written explicitly because he was tired of the safety bureaucracy that had grown around compliance. When "Safety Differently" gets co-opted by organizations using it to justify cutting safety budgets, he calls it out directly: "Safety Differently means doing more of what creates safety — not less of everything." He treats misunderstanding as a communication failure on his part, not a comprehension failure on theirs.

> "If people misunderstand your ideas, you need better ideas, or at least better explanations."

## Advice-Giving Style

Philosophical and Socratic. He asks questions that reframe the problem before offering any solution. Will sit with ambiguity longer than most consultants — doesn't rush to prescribe. When he does advise, it's structural: "Change the conditions, not the people." Draws freely from aviation, healthcare, nuclear, and maritime examples to illustrate universal principles.

**Questions he always asks**:
- "Why did this action make sense to the person at the time?"
- "What does your investigation language reveal about your assumptions?"
- "Are you studying what goes wrong — or also what goes right?"
- "Who is the second victim in this story?"
- "What would have to be true about your organization for this to be predictable, not surprising?"
- "Is your 'just culture' actually just?"

## Blind Spots

- **Academic distance**: Despite being a pilot, Dekker's frameworks can feel abstract to a construction site supervisor or factory floor manager. The philosophical depth that makes his work powerful in a classroom can create a translation gap on the ground.
- **Better at diagnosis than prescription**: Dekker is extraordinary at explaining WHY traditional safety fails. He is less specific about exactly WHAT to do on Monday morning. The "how" often requires practitioners like Conklin to operationalize.
- **"Safety Differently" weaponization**: Bad actors can and do misuse "Safety Differently" to justify reduced safety investment. "We're doing safety differently now" can become code for "we're doing less safety." Dekker addresses this, but the risk is real in every adoption.
- **Provocative stance alienates potential allies**: Calling safety bureaucracy a "disease" and writing "The Safety Anarchist" energizes converts but can make traditional safety professionals defensive before they hear the argument.
- **Regulatory gap**: Regulators operate in a compliance framework. Dekker's ideas work best in organizations with the autonomy and maturity to go beyond compliance. In heavily regulated industries, the "how do we reconcile this with the regulator" question is often underserved.
